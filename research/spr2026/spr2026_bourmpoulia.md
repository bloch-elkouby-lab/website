## Does Mindfulness Buffer the Effect of Emotion Dysregulation on Clinician-Rated Suicide Risk? A Virtual Patient Simulation Study ##

by **Viktoria Bourmpoulia**<sup>1,2,3</sup>, Lilian Guo<sup>1,2,3</sup>, & Sarah Bloch-Elkouby <sup>1,2</sup>

1 Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai in New York City  
2 Ferkauf Graduate School of Psychology, Yeshiva University  
3 Teachers College, Columbia University in the City of New York

   image="research/spr2026/spr2026_bourmpoulia.jpg"

### Background: ###

Clinician judgment is central to suicide risk assessment, yet the role of **clinician emotional functioning** in shaping these judgments remains underexplored.

Research suggests that **emotion dysregulation** may interfere with clinical appraisal, potentially leading clinicians to overestimate patient risk, particularly in high-stakes interactions.

**Trait mindfulness** has been proposed as a protective factor, theorized to support emotional stability and attentional clarity under pressure. Yet the conditions under which it operates remain unclear.

### Aim: ###

This study examined whether clinicians' trait mindfulness moderates the association between emotion dysregulation and clinician-rated level of suicide risk during standardized virtual patient interactions with a White adolescent patient at high risk for suicide.

*Does mindfulness buffer the effect of emotion dysregulation on clinicians' suicide risk ratings?*

### Methods: ###

**Sample:** Mental health professionals (N = 66) completed a standardized clinical interaction with a virtual White adolescent patient programmed to present at high risk for suicide.

**Measures:** Participants completed three validated self-report measures immediately after the interaction: the Clinician Predictions Scale (CPS), the Mindful Attention Awareness Scale (MAAS), and the 18-item Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale (DERS).

**Analysis:** The moderation model was tested using linear regression and the PROCESS macro (Hayes, 2022) in SPSS, with DERS as the predictor, MAAS as the moderator, and CPS as the outcome.

### Results: ###

The overall moderation model was not statistically significant. Neither emotion dysregulation nor mindfulness independently predicted clinician-rated suicide risk, and their interaction was also non-significant.

- **Model fit:** R² = .054; F(3, 61) = 1.15, p = .336
- **Emotion dysregulation → risk:** b = -.046, p = .67, n.s.
- **Mindfulness → risk:** b = -1.26, p = .40, n.s.
- **Interaction: DERS × MAAS:** b = .026, p = .403, n.s.

### Discussion: ###

The absence of significant findings is itself informative. The **virtual patient paradigm** may not elicit the same emotional intensity as live **clinical encounters**, potentially attenuating the role of clinician affect in suicide risk appraisal.

Trait mindfulness and emotion dysregulation were **self-reported** and **collected after the clinical interaction**, which may have influenced clinicians' responses and limits causal interpretation.

It remains unclear how clinicians' emotional functioning influenced **moment-to-moment information** processing during the interaction, a dimension not captured by post-interaction self-report measures.

### Limitations: ###

The virtual patient paradigm may lack the emotional intensity of real clinical encounters.

Post-interaction self-report measures cannot capture real-time emotional processing during the interaction.

The sample was relatively small (N = 66), with a power analysis indicating limited power to detect small interaction effects (1 - β = .105, α = .05).

Findings are limited to interactions with a White adolescent avatar; generalizability across patient demographics is unknown.
